How do daily habits shape mental health?

We explore how everyday lifestyle behaviors — sleep, exercise, diet, and screen time — relate to stress levels, happiness, and mental health conditions in a global survey of 3,000 individuals. As students ourselves, we are particularly interested in patterns among young adults and what lifestyle choices might support better mental well-being.

Dataset

About the data

Our dataset was sourced from Kaggle and is a collection of global survey responses related to lifestyle habits and mental well-being. It contains 3,000 observations and 12 variables, with each row representing one individual's survey response. The data draws from international surveys and research studies examining factors associated with mental health, daily behaviors, and overall well-being. Since the dataset is observational, we focus on identifying patterns and correlations rather than establishing causation.

Visualization 1 — D3.js
Frequency of mental health conditions

A bar chart showing the count of each reported mental health condition across all 3,000 respondents. This overview establishes the landscape of mental health in our dataset before exploring lifestyle correlations.

ℹ️ How to interact: Hover over any bar to see the exact count and percentage of respondents with that condition. The hovered bar will dim to highlight your selection.
Takeaway: Mental health conditions are distributed nearly equally across the four categories — Anxiety (628), PTSD (624), Depression (580), and Bipolar Disorder (573). Anxiety is the most frequently reported condition. The even distribution suggests this dataset captures a broad cross-section of mental health experiences rather than skewing toward one diagnosis, making it a well-balanced foundation for further analysis.
Visualization 2 — Matplotlib
Sleep hours by diet type

Box plots showing the distribution of nightly sleep hours across five dietary patterns: Balanced, Junk Food, Keto, Vegan, and Vegetarian. This examines whether dietary habits are reflected in sleep duration.

Takeaway: Sleep distributions are broadly similar across all five diet types, with median sleep hours ranging narrowly between 6.3 and 6.6 hours. Junk Food and Vegetarian diets show the highest medians, while Balanced diets show the lowest — a counterintuitive result. The substantial overlap across all groups suggests that diet type alone is not a strong predictor of sleep duration.
Visualization 3 — Matplotlib
Sleep hours by happiness level

Box plots comparing sleep hours across three happiness level categories — Low (score 0–4), Medium (4–7), and High (7–10). This examines whether happier individuals tend to sleep more.

Takeaway: Individuals with high happiness scores show a modestly higher median sleep duration compared to those with low happiness. However, the distributions overlap substantially across all three groups, indicating that sleep alone does not determine happiness — it plays a supporting role alongside other lifestyle factors.
Visualization 4 — Altair
Happiness, stress & exercise — interactive dashboard (women)

A three-panel Altair dashboard combining: happiness by stress level, exercise by happiness faceted by stress, and stress by happiness faceted by exercise. A shared dropdown filter links all three panels.

ℹ️ How to interact: Use the dropdown menu at the top of the chart to filter all three panels by stress level (Low, Moderate, or High). This lets you compare exercise–happiness patterns within each stress group simultaneously.
Takeaway: Median happiness scores are strikingly similar across all three stress levels, suggesting stress alone does not strongly predict happiness. In contrast, exercise level shows a clearer positive relationship with happiness across all stress conditions — higher exercise consistently corresponds to higher happiness scores regardless of stress.
Visualization 5 — Altair
Happiness distribution by exercise level — strip plot (women)

A jittered strip plot where each dot represents one woman in the dataset. Points are spread horizontally with jitter to reduce overlap and reveal the true density of happiness scores within each exercise group.

ℹ️ How to interact: Use the dropdown to filter points by stress level. Hover over individual dots to highlight them and see their exact exercise level, happiness score, and stress level in a tooltip.
Takeaway: Happiness scores are widely distributed within each exercise group, reflecting high individual variability. However, individuals with high exercise levels appear more frequently in the upper range of happiness scores. This pattern holds consistently across all stress levels, reinforcing that physical activity has a stable positive association with well-being independent of stress.
Visualization 6 — Altair
Average happiness by stress & happiness distribution by exercise (women)

A side-by-side layout: a bar chart showing average happiness per stress level on the left, and a histogram of happiness score distributions filtered by exercise level on the right.

ℹ️ How to interact: Hover over bars in the left chart to highlight them individually. On the right chart, use the dropdown to switch between Low, Moderate, and High exercise levels and observe how the happiness distribution shifts.
Takeaway: Average happiness differs by less than 0.5 points across all stress levels, confirming that stress level is not a strong standalone determinant of happiness. Meanwhile, the histogram shows that higher exercise levels shift the distribution of happiness scores upward — more respondents fall in the 6–9 range for high-exercise groups compared to low-exercise groups.
Visualization 7 — Altair
Average happiness heatmap — stress × exercise level (women)

A heatmap showing average happiness at every combination of stress level and exercise level. Color intensity (viridis scale — darker = lower, lighter = higher) encodes the average happiness value, making interaction effects easy to spot at a glance.

ℹ️ How to interact: Hover over any cell to see the exact average happiness score for that stress–exercise combination.
Takeaway: Higher exercise levels are associated with higher average happiness across all stress levels. Notably, the combination of low exercise and low stress yields a surprisingly low average happiness — suggesting physical activity is independently important for well-being, not merely a stress buffer. For high-stress individuals, increasing exercise appears to partially offset stress's negative effect on happiness.
Visualization 8 — Altair
Happiness density by stress level — violin plot (women)

A violin/density plot showing the full distribution shape of happiness scores for each stress level, filterable by exercise level. Unlike box plots, this reveals the entire shape and spread of each group's happiness distribution.

ℹ️ How to interact: Use the dropdown to filter by exercise level (Low, Moderate, or High). Observe how the density curves shift across stress groups as you change the exercise filter.
Takeaway: When filtered to high exercise levels, happiness density curves shift toward higher values compared to low exercise across all stress groups. Distributions for low and moderate stress overlap substantially regardless of exercise level, but high-exercise individuals show a distribution more concentrated in the mid-to-high happiness range, further supporting exercise as a key lifestyle factor for well-being.
Conclusion

What we learned

Our analysis of 3,000 global survey responses reveals consistent patterns about which lifestyle habits are most meaningfully associated with mental well-being. While no single factor determines happiness or mental health, physical activity stands out as the most robust and consistent predictor across all visualizations.

Exercise is the strongest predictor of happiness

Across every visualization, higher exercise levels consistently correspond to higher happiness scores — regardless of stress level, diet, or other factors. This was the most robust pattern in the entire dataset.

Stress level alone doesn't determine happiness

Average happiness differed by less than 0.5 points across low, moderate, and high stress groups. How people manage stress — particularly through exercise — appears to matter more than the stress level itself.

Mental health conditions are broadly distributed

Anxiety, PTSD, Depression, and Bipolar Disorder are nearly equally represented across all 3,000 respondents, suggesting these conditions cut across demographic and lifestyle groups rather than clustering in specific ones.

Sleep and diet show modest associations

Higher happiness is associated with slightly more sleep, but the effect is small. Diet type shows minimal differences in sleep hours. These factors may play a supporting role rather than a primary one in mental well-being.

Future directions

Since this dataset is observational, we cannot establish causation. Future work could use longitudinal data to track how changes in habits correspond to changes in well-being over time. It would also be valuable to include more granular measures — such as exercise type and intensity, sleep quality rather than just duration, and dietary quantity alongside type. Additionally, expanding analysis beyond women to compare all gender groups would strengthen the generalizability of these findings. Interventional studies targeting exercise as a low-cost, accessible mental health support strategy would be a particularly impactful next step.
